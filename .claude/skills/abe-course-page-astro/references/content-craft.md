# Content craft — turning a section brief into prose

Stage 4's job is narrow and specific: take one section brief and write the section. Not "write the
page". The brief already decided what the section claims, who is reading it, what objection it
defuses and what research finding should shape it. If you find yourself inventing any of that while
writing, stop and go back to Stage 3 — the brief was incomplete, and writing over the gap is how
generic copy gets made.

This file is the positive half of the rules. The prohibitions live in `authority-and-seo-rules.md`.
Copy written only to avoid tripwires reads defensively; these are the moves that make it read like
someone who knows the subject wrote it.

## The three failures this prevents

1. **Restating the fact.** The ledger says the threshold is a number. The section says the threshold
   is a number. Nothing has been added, and the reader could have got that from the regulator.
2. **Spending the research nowhere.** Stage 2 found that no competitor states the CPD category. If the
   section opens the way it would have opened without that finding, the research was wasted.
3. **House-style filler.** Sentences that could appear on any page of any provider: "we understand
   that compliance can be confusing", "our courses are designed with you in mind". Delete on sight.

## Move 1 — Translate the fact, do not restate it

A verified fact is raw material. The section's job is to answer the reader's question with it.

The move: **state the fact, then immediately say what it means for this reader.** The second half is
the writing; the first half is just accuracy.

**Weak (restates):**
> The owner builder approval threshold in Queensland is $11,000. Projects above this value require
> approval from the QBCC.

**Better (translates):**
> If your project is worth more than $11,000 and you are not using a licensed builder, you need QBCC
> owner builder approval before work starts. Under that figure, you do not, and this course is not
> something you have to buy.

Same fact. The second version answers the question the reader arrived with, and it tells the reader
below the threshold that they can leave. That costs a sale occasionally and buys trust every time.

## Move 2 — Spend the distinctive material

Every brief carries a `Distinctive material` line: the thing Stage 2 found that competitors miss. It
must visibly change the section, usually by changing what the section leads with.

Ask of every finished section: **if I deleted the research finding, would this section be different?**
If not, it has not been spent. Rewrite the opening so the gap is the first thing the reader gets,
because being first to answer the unanswered question is the entire competitive position.

**Finding:** competitors state CPD points but never the category, and category is what practitioners
are audited on.

**Not spent:** "This course is worth 2 CPD points and falls under the Building Law category."
**Spent:** "This course counts as Building Law, which is the category most practitioners are short on
at audit. It carries 2 points."

## Move 3 — Write the capsule as an answer, not an introduction

40 to 60 words, answer first. The test: **if the reader read only the capsule and left, would they
have their answer?** If the capsule sets up an answer that arrives in the following paragraph, it is
an introduction and it has failed.

Introductions look like: "There are a few things to consider when…", "Understanding your obligations
is an important part of…", "Many people ask whether…". None of these answer anything. Cut them and
start at the answer.

Keep roughly 120 to 180 words between headings. If a section runs long, it is usually two sections.

## Move 4 — Name the objection out loud

The brief states the objection the section defuses. The strongest treatment is to say it plainly in
the reader's own words and then answer it, rather than tiptoeing around it.

> Providers differ only in whether they are an RTO, and that makes no difference to what the QBCC
> accepts. There is no accredited version of this course to compare against.

That is more convincing than three paragraphs of reassurance, because it engages the actual doubt.
This works only when the answer is genuinely strong; if it is not, the fix is the offer, not the copy.

## Voice

Write for a capable adult who is short of time and slightly worried about getting something wrong.

- **Plain and specific.** Prefer the concrete noun and the real number to the category word. "The
  QBCC charges $364.65" beats "government fees apply".
- **Unhedged.** If the fact is verified, state it. "May", "generally", "in most cases" belong only
  where there genuinely is variation, and then name the variation rather than gesturing at it.
- **Second person, active voice.** "You apply to the Building Services Board", not "an application is
  made".
- **Short sentences carrying one idea.** Vary the length so it does not read as a list.
- **No enthusiasm.** This reader is discharging an obligation, not buying a holiday. Warmth comes from
  being useful and honest, not from exclamation.
- **Say the unhelpful thing.** Who does not need the course, what the total actually costs including
  the fees ABE does not receive, what the course does not cover. This is the single most reliable
  trust signal available and competitors will not copy it.
- Australian English. No em dashes in body copy. Never "comprehensive".

## Move 5 — The cold reread

Do this before the section plan, not after the build. At Stage 7 a rewrite is expensive and the tools
are structural; here it costs nothing.

Read the section back as the reader in the brief's "arrives" line, and check:

1. **The fail condition.** Every brief has one. Has it been met?
2. **The delete test.** Cut the research finding. Does the section change? If not, spend it.
3. **The first-sentence test.** Does sentence one answer something, or set up an answer?
4. **The anywhere test.** Could this sentence appear on a competitor's page unchanged? If yes, it is
   carrying no information. Replace it with something only ABE can say.
5. **The fact-to-meaning test.** Every stated fact followed by what it means for this reader.
6. **The archetype test.** Does anything here belong to a different archetype? Check the forbidden
   carry-overs list for the one you selected.
7. **Sources.** Every government fact carries its source and verified date. Any internal fact still
   unconfirmed is a Stage 1 failure, not a `[confirm:]` marker.

## Worked example — brief to draft

**Brief:**
```
Section: Does this course count towards my CPD?
Claim: Counts towards [regulator]'s CPD requirement, Building Law category, 2 points.
Reader arrives: needs points, unsure this one qualifies, mildly sceptical.
Objection defused: "I'll do it and then find out it doesn't count."
Distinctive material: competitors state points but not category; category is
       what practitioners are audited on.
Fails if: the answer is not complete within the capsule, or the category is
       stated without its source.
```

**First draft, weak:**
> Continuing professional development is an important requirement for licensed builders in Tasmania.
> This course has been designed to help you meet your obligations. It is worth 2 CPD points and covers
> important topics in building law. Enrol today to keep your licence current.

Everything wrong at once: opens with a definition the reader does not need, spends the research
nowhere, states points without category or source, and closes with a CTA inside the answer block.

**Rewritten:**
> This course counts as Building Law under CBOS Tasmania's CPD requirement, and carries 2 points.
> Building Law is the category most practitioners come up short on at audit, because it is the
> hardest to accumulate incidentally on the job. Completing it is recorded against your licence
> automatically.
>
> ✓ VERIFIED · 🔗 SOURCES — CBOS CPD requirements, verified [date]

Answer complete in the capsule. Category leads, because that is the gap. The reason the category
matters is stated, which is the translation move. Source carried. No CTA in the answer block.
