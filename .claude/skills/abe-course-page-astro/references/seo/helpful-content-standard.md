# Helpful Content Standard — people-first requirements for ABE pages

**When to read:** Step 4 (while drafting any page) and Step 6 (Category P). Read alongside `seo-strategy.md`, which remains the strategy backbone — this file is the *self-assessment* layer beneath it.

**Verified:** 20 July 2026 — checked line-by-line against the full text of Google's "Creating helpful, reliable, people-first content". **Re-verify:** with the freshness audit cadence (🟡 volatility — the doctrine is stable, the phrasing shifts).

---

## 1. Where the helpful content system stands

There is no longer a standalone "Helpful Content Update" signal. In the March 2024 core update Google folded the helpful-content classifier into its **core ranking systems**, so helpfulness is assessed **continuously as part of core ranking**, not by a separate penalty. Google's page is written as a **self-assessment checklist, not a rule list**. Treat it as the practical bar for every ABE page.

**Google's own method for using it** — worth mirroring, because ABE already has the machinery:
- Self-assess against the questions below.
- **Have someone unaffiliated with the site assess it honestly.** ABE's independent named-reviewer gate already satisfies this; treat the reviewer as a genuine outside check, not a byline.
- **Audit real drops.** Which pages lost ground, on which queries — then read those pages against these questions. This is what Step 7.5 rank tracking is for.

## 2. The requirements, as self-assessment questions

**Content and quality.** Original information, reporting, research or analysis? Insightful analysis *beyond the obvious*? Complete coverage of the topic? Where it draws on other sources, does it add substantial value rather than rewriting them? Does the **H1 / page title describe the content accurately without exaggerating or sensationalising**? Would a reader bookmark or share it? Would it stand up in a printed reference work? Does it carry **substantial value against the other pages already ranking** for the query? Free of spelling and stylistic problems? Produced with care rather than sloppily or hastily? Not mass-produced across a network with little attention to any one page?

**Expertise.** Clear sourcing and visible evidence of the expertise involved? Background about the author and about the site itself (author page, About page)? Would someone researching ABE come away seeing it as well-trusted on its topic? Written or reviewed by someone who demonstrably knows the subject? Free of easily-verified factual errors?

**People-first tests.** Four questions that catch what the quality checklist misses:
- **The direct-visit test.** Would the intended audience find this useful if they came straight to ABE without a search engine? If the page only makes sense as a search landing target, it fails.
- **First-hand expertise**, which Google defines concretely as the kind that comes from **actually having used the thing or been to the place** — not from reading about it. See §5.
- **The goal test.** After reading, has the person learned enough to actually achieve what they came for?
- **The satisfaction test.** Do they leave feeling the visit was worth it?

**The who / how / why test.**
- **Who** made it — self-evident authorship; bylines where a reader would expect one; bylines that lead to real author detail covering their background and subject areas.
- **How** it was made — transparency about method (see §2a).
- **Why** it exists — created primarily to help people, not to attract search visits. Google calls this the most important of the three.

### 2a. The "How" question applied to an AI content engine

This section matters more for ABE than for most publishers, because **this skill is an AI content engine**. Google's position:

- Automated, AI-generated and AI-assisted content all have a "How" component.
- Where automation substantially generates content, ask: is its use **self-evident to visitors**; is there **background on how** it was used; is there an explanation of **why** it was useful here?
- Disclosure is warranted wherever a reader might reasonably think *"how was this made?"*
- **Confirmed live 20 July 2026 — Google's spam-policy scope was formally expanded 15 May 2026.** The top-level definition of spam now explicitly names "attempting to manipulate generative AI responses in Google Search" alongside manipulating rankings. This is not a new item in the list; it is a scope change to the definition of spam itself, meaning every category below — scaled content, hidden text, cloaking — covers AI Overview/AI Mode manipulation as directly as it covers classic rankings.

Using automation **primarily to manipulate rankings is a spam-policy violation** (scaled content abuse) — not merely a quality problem. Google points to the **Search Quality Rater Guidelines §4.6.5** (scaled content abuse) and **§4.6.6** (main content created with little effort, originality or added value) as the descriptions of what this looks like in practice. Read those two sections if a page's originality is ever genuinely in question.
- **The fan-out trap.** Generating a separate page for every query variation a model might fan out to — precisely the tactic an SEO engine is tempted toward — is named by Google as violating the same policy when done to influence rankings or AI responses. One consolidated authoritative page beats many thin variants; page count is not quality.
- **Auto-generated metadata counts as content.** Titles, meta descriptions, structured data and alt text produced by automation are held to the same accuracy standard as the body, and structured data must match the visible text.

**ABE ruling:** the existing "how this was developed and reviewed" note is the mechanism. On substantive pages it should state, in plain terms, that content is drafted against verified regulatory sources and reviewed by a named expert before publication. Volume is never the justification — the second AI-content governance gate (named-human-expert review) is what keeps ABE on the right side of the scaled-content line.

**Search-engine-first warning signs.** Content made mainly to attract search visits; producing across many topics hoping some rank; **extensive automation across many topics**; summarising what others say without adding value; chasing trending subjects ABE would not otherwise cover; **leaving the reader needing to search again** to get a better answer elsewhere; writing to a target word count (Google confirms it has no preferred count); entering a niche with no real expertise; **promising an answer to a question that has no confirmed answer** (a fee, date or rule that has not actually been set); **artificially freshening pages by changing the date**; and bulk-adding or bulk-removing content to make the site seem fresh.

## 3. ABE ruling — YMYL and the trust bar

Among Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness and Trust, **trust is the most important**. The other three contribute to trust, and **content does not have to demonstrate all of them** — some pages earn their place on demonstrated experience, others on expertise. E-E-A-T is **not itself a ranking factor**; Google's systems use a mix of signals that tend to identify content with strong E-E-A-T. Treat it as a diagnosis, not a checklist item to tick.

Google gives **more weight to strong E-E-A-T on YMYL topics** — those that could significantly affect people's **health, financial stability or safety, or the welfare and well-being of society**.

**ABE ruling:** owner-builder, CPD and White Card content is **YMYL**, on Google's own terms and not by analogy. It governs whether someone may lawfully build, what they must spend, and whether they and others go home safe from a site. Therefore:

- The trust signals in `quality-gates.md` §6 are **not optional** on these pages.
- An unsourced or undated regulatory claim is a **publish hard blocker** (already the rule — this is the reason for it).
- Authority-model honesty (`authority-model.md`) is a **trust** requirement, not merely a compliance one.
- The **search quality rater guidelines** are the long-form expansion of what raters look for on YMYL pages; read them when a trust question is genuinely contested, not routinely.

## 4. House style — the "comprehensive" reconciliation

Google's checklist asks for a substantial, complete or *comprehensive* description of the topic. **ABE house style bans that word.** There is no conflict: hit the same bar and write it as **thorough, complete coverage**. The intent is identical; only the label changes. Never reintroduce the banned word to satisfy the checklist.

## 5. Showing experience, not asserting it

The weakest dimension on ABE pages. An abstract claim ("31,000+ students trained") is an assertion. Google defines the experience it rewards as the kind that comes from **having actually used the product or service, or been to the place** — so the page has to show the doing. On a course page, show at least two of:

- **What actually happens after you enrol** — a short walk-through of the real experience, not a generic stepper.
- **Measured figures, not soft phrases.** Use the platform's real completion data (e.g. "about five and a half hours", from a 322-minute average) rather than "a few hours". Check LearnWorlds' course overview before writing any duration.
- **The real course environment** — a genuine screenshot, not a generated or stock image.
- **The specific artefacts a student ends up with or lodges** — name the actual form, certificate or number.
- **Named, specific outcomes** tied to the jurisdiction, not generic reassurance.

**The pattern Google points to elsewhere** — in its product-review guidance it asks for evidence of the work: how many things were tested, what the results were, how the testing was done, with photographs as proof. The transferable principle is **evidence of the work, not a claim about it**.

**The formula, from three published Australian examples** (a Perth patio-design page built on 26+ years of local jobs; an ankle-physio page with credentials, per-exercise photos, rep counts and safety modifications; an eye-health page with prominent credentials and plain-language definitions):

> **experience you actually have + a named expert + specifics only you can provide + care for the reader's decision.**

## 6. Anti-patterns — what fails the bar

- The same course copy with **only the state name swapped** (mass-produced / little added value; also a cannibalisation fail).
- A page that lists **course features but never answers "do I actually need this, and is it legit?"**
- A page that leaves the reader **needing to search again** — the single most useful failure test in the whole checklist. If the obvious next question is unanswered, the page is not finished.
- An **H1 or meta title that oversells** — exaggerated or shock phrasing fails the quality questions outright, regardless of character count.
- **Stating something as settled when it is not** — a fee, commencement date or rule that has not actually been confirmed. Mark it unverified or leave it out.
- An **undated regulatory claim**, or a fee with **no source** (trust failure on a YMYL topic).
- A **wall of unattributed text with no author** (fails "who").
- **Undisclosed automation** where a reader would reasonably ask how the page was made (fails "how"; at scale, a spam-policy risk).
- **Spelling and style defects.** Named explicitly in Google's quality questions — which is why Category D and the `final-check` skill are quality gates, not polish.
- **Date-freshening.** An "Updated [Month YYYY]" line must reflect a real content update, and a per-fact `Verified [date]` must reflect a real re-verification against the source. **Never bump a date to look current.** Understating freshness is safe; overstating it is a trust failure. The same applies to bulk content churn done to look fresh.

## 7. Page experience is part of this

Google's core systems reward good page experience, and warn against fixing one or two aspects while ignoring the rest. For ABE this is not a separate workstream — it is already covered by `seo-strategy.md` §13 (Core Web Vitals) and the `abe-readability-audit` skill (measure, contrast, scanning, layout). A page that clears every content question but is slow, cramped or hard to read has not cleared the bar.

## 8. On SEO itself

Google states plainly that SEO is a **helpful activity when applied to people-first content**, and a problem only when the content is search-engine-first. This is the primary-source backing for the engine's existing "AI search is SEO done well" doctrine. Optimising a genuinely useful ABE page is not a compromise; producing a page that exists to rank is.

## 9. The page checklist

A page clears the bar when it:

1. Answers the reader's real question first, and covers the topic fully (not padded to a word count).
2. Leaves no obvious next question that sends the reader back to search.
3. Adds something only ABE can — original analysis, first-hand experience, specifics.
4. Shows experience rather than asserting it (≥2 signals from §5).
5. Names its author **and** independent reviewer, with dates and profile links.
6. Sources every government/legal fact to the `.gov.au` original, with a visible last-verified date.
7. States the authority model honestly (no overclaiming), and discloses how it was developed and reviewed.
8. Is genuinely state-specific, not swapped copy.
9. Carries an H1 and meta title that describe the page accurately without overselling.
10. Reads as carefully produced — clear headings, clean spelling and style, no easily-checked errors, no artificial date-freshening.

Items 5–7 are where regulatory/educational sites win or lose, because trust dominates E-E-A-T for YMYL. They map one-to-one onto existing ABE components (`Credentials`, `VerifiedSources`, `SourcesFooter`, the authority-model attestation).
